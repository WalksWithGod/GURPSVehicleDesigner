   In April 1992, the Infinity Corporation approached its long time partner, Fulcrum Aerospace, with an unusual request. Infinity wanted Fulcrum to build them an aircraft carrier. Fulcrum's CEO, Maxwell Voss, had just initiated a program of modernization and development of advanced materials, systems, and manufacturing techniques. He saw Infinity's request as an opportunity to showcase the results of the new program.

    The initial design was completed in October 1996. The following fifteen months were spent refining the design with representatives of Infinity. The keel was laid for ICS Infinity Prototype (ACN 1) in January 1998 at Fulcrum Shipbuilding in Hoquiam, Washington. Building of the ship went fairly smoothly during the early phases. The only major problem in those early phases occurred during the summer of 1998, when temperatures soared into the 90s, causing several workers to be hospitalized for heat prostration. Work was halted for a few days. Fortunately, the heat wave broke and work could continue.

    The most serious threat to the project came in May 1999, during the systems integration phase of construction. Fulcrum was trying to obtain the fuel rods for the Prototype's reactors. The US government was questioning Infinity's need for an aircraft carrier, especially one that was nuclear-powered. After six months, representatives of more than twenty government agencies met with the leaders of the Loslore Consortium, which included Infinity president Warren Sharpe and Maxwell Voss. The exact nature of the meeting is not known as the records are classified, but a week later, all permits were approved.

    Work progressed smoothly and the Prototype was launched on August 2, 2001. After a successful series of sea trials, she was delivered to Infinity on August 10, 2002. Fulcrum decided to build three more of the vessels for their own use. The FSS William Fulcrum II (ACN 2) was laid in October 1999. Enough had been learned during the construction of the Prototype to reduce the building time and thus Fulcrum II finished its sea trials on the same day that the Prototype was delivered. The third-of-class, FSS William Fulcrum III (ACN 3) (sometimes referred to as the Billy to avoid confusion with Fulcrum II), was laid in June 2000. The fourth ship, FSS Adam Jones (ACN 4), was laid in January 2001. Fulcrum III is expected to be completed early in 2003 and the Jones in late 2003.

Devastator CIWS

This defensive weapon system is a self-contained turret module. The only requirements for mounting it are space for the turret's base and a power supply. A connection to an onboard computer is optional. It has the following features:

Weapon: Fulcrum Armsworks I20 20mm smoothbore 4-barrel electric Gatling gun that fires APFSDS rounds. Mounted in a universal mount. Magazine holds 4000 rounds.

Sensor: TL8 AESA with 10 mile range.

Computer: TL8 compact genius minicomputer (complexity 4) running a Gunner program with optional Datalink software to allow manual control and access to transmission profiling software.

Structure & Armor: The turret has a light standard frame with standard composite armor (DR 10 PD 3). It is sealed to withstand the elements.

CL3/L200

This missile launch system consists of 3 linked L200 launch tubes. Like the Devastator, the CL3/L200 is modular. The launchers are universal mounted on an open mount. Although any firing combination may be used, the standard method of firing is to launch 1 missile every second. This gives the system an effective rate of fire of 1 even though the individual launcher has a RoF of 1/3. The following are details of the system:
